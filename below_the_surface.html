01 / 06
fear of failing the people i love
what it is

this isn't standard fear of abandonment, exactly. it's more specific: i have an overwhelming need to not be the reason someone is inconvenienced, upset, or disappointed. when i sense that i've caused even minor frustration — a wrong tone, a small ask at a bad time — i don't get angry or defensive. i disappear.

why i think it happens

i think i learned very early that being a burden is a moral failure. that taking up space — needing things — makes me a problem. so when i sense friction, my instinct is to make myself as small and invisible as possible until the danger passes.

what helps
do
  • soften your tone when you notice i've gone quiet
  • say "i'm not upset with you" without being asked
  • give me a few minutes, then gently check in
  • let me know when you're frustrated at a situation, not me
don't
  • sigh heavily or use clipped responses when i've asked for something
  • go silent immediately after i've said something — i'll assume the worst
  • expect me to "just know" you're fine when your tone says otherwise

02 / 06
conflict makes me disappear
what it is

when there's conflict — or when someone goes quiet on me unexpectedly — i don't pick one reaction. i do all of them at once. i shut down. i get anxious. i mask it completely and pretend i'm fine. then i obsessively replay the conversation looking for what i did wrong.

i will not tell you i'm hurting. i will smile. i will say "i'm okay." i will carry it alone until it becomes too heavy, and by then you might not even know anything happened. i go non-verbal sometimes — not as a choice, just as a fact. words stop coming.

why i think it happens

expressing hurt feels like accusing someone. and accusing someone feels like risking everything. so i swallow it. it feels safer to suffer quietly than to say "you hurt me" and watch something change.

what helps
do
  • reach out first if something feels off between us
  • make it easy to say "that actually hurt" without consequences
  • be patient when i go quiet — don't push, just stay
  • tell me directly if you're okay, even if it seems obvious
don't
  • wait for me to bring up conflict first — i likely never will
  • interpret my silence as indifference. it's usually the opposite.
  • dismiss small hurts as "not a big deal" — to me, they compound

03 / 06
i perform versions of myself
what it is

i shift a lot depending on who i'm with. not lying — more like tuning. around some people i'm loud and playful. around others i'm quieter, more careful, watching what i say. this happens a lot, and i don't always realize i'm doing it.

the version of me you first meet might feel different from the me that shows up three months in. as i get more comfortable, the mask thins. what's underneath is softer, messier, and more real — but also more easily hurt.

why i think it happens

i've learned that showing the full version of me too fast tends to overwhelm people or push them away. so i reveal slowly, in layers, testing safety. the irony is that the people i feel safest with are the ones most likely to see the parts of me i can't fully control.

what helps
do
  • let me be different around you than i am elsewhere
  • notice when i get more real and meet that with gentleness
  • reassure me occasionally that you like who i actually am
don't
  • compare "fun Katie" to "quiet Katie" like one is wrong
  • push me to "just be myself" — i'm trying. it takes time.
  • act surprised when i reveal something heavy. i've been holding it a while.

04 / 06
i am convinced i am too much
what it is

i hold back. constantly. there are things i want to say, feelings i want to express, help i want to ask for — and i don't. because some part of me genuinely believes that if i show too much need, you'll leave.

i feel like a burden as a default. not as a passing thought — as a baseline assumption about who i am in relation to other people. i assume my needs are inconvenient. i assume my emotions are exhausting. i assume that if i asked for what i actually needed, you'd quietly start pulling away.

why i think it happens

i think this is built from a long history of reading the room and deciding i was reading as "too much." whether that was accurate or not, the pattern stuck. needing things = being a problem. so i edit myself before i speak.

what helps
do
  • invite me to share. "you can tell me, i want to know."
  • respond to small needs warmly so i learn it's safe to have them
  • tell me explicitly that i'm not a burden — unprompted
  • notice when i'm clearly holding something back and make space
don't
  • respond to small needs with sighs, delays, or reluctance
  • make me feel like i'm interrupting your life by needing something
  • only check in when things are obviously wrong

05 / 06
dysphoria — body, voice, and presence
what it is

i'm a transfem girl. that means some days i look in the mirror or hear my voice and it all feels wrong. not sad-wrong. jarring-wrong. like the outside doesn't match the inside, and there's nothing i can do about it in that moment.

some days it's fine. some days it isn't. it doesn't follow a pattern i can predict. it can be triggered by a photo, a recording, seeing myself in a video call, or just a passing thought. i also carry anxiety about what other people think — whether they see me as who i am, whether they're clocking me, whether i'm "passing" or just existing awkwardly in the wrong skin.

what helps
do
  • use she/her consistently, especially around others
  • affirm me — compliments go a long way on hard days
  • if i say i'm dysphoric, just acknowledge it. you don't need to fix it
  • treat my gender as fact, not something to tiptoe around
don't
  • make jokes about my voice or appearance, even small ones
  • bring up pre-transition anything unless i bring it up first
  • say "you don't look dysphoric" — that's not how it works
  • go quiet after i mention it, like it's uncomfortable

06 / 06
closeness makes me more vulnerable, not less
what it is

most people assume getting closer to someone means getting more comfortable — more resilient, less reactive. for me it's the opposite. the closer you get, the lower my guard drops. which means the more impact you have.

a stranger ignoring me barely registers. a close friend doing the same thing can ruin my whole day. this isn't me being dramatic. it's just that the people i care about most are also the people with the most access to actually hurt me — and i let them have that access because i love them.

i also give a lot quietly. i notice things. i remember things. i do small careful things to make the people i love feel seen. i don't usually announce it. i just do it. and i don't need it returned in the same way — i just need to know it's noticed sometimes.

what helps
do
  • be consistent. consistent more than anything else.
  • acknowledge the small things i do — i notice that you noticed
  • remind me occasionally that you're not going anywhere
  • treat teasing as something that requires care the closer we are
don't
  • go from warm to distant without explanation
  • assume i know how you feel — say it
  • mistake my quiet giving for not needing anything in return
  • think the teasing that felt fine a month ago still feels the same now
